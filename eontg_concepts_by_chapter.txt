CH 01 - THE ALPHABET
================================================================
Focus
  Greek alphabet, pronunciation, breathings, iota subscript,
  punctuation, basic accent recognition.

Key items
  - Capital and lower-case letters; their order and names.
  - Vowels (long, short, dichronous) and the seven diphthongs.
  - Rough breathing and smooth breathing on every vowel-initial
    word and on initial rho.
  - Iota subscript (a, h, w with subscript): silent in
    pronunciation but grammatically real.
  - Punctuation: comma (,), full stop (.), raised dot for English
    semicolon, Greek semicolon (;) for English question mark.
  - Recognition of acute, grave, and circumflex accents.

Drill targets
  - Read aloud unfamiliar Greek words.
  - Transliterate short Greek words into Latin script and back.
  - Spot missing breathings or iota subscripts in student output.

Common errors
  - Forgetting breathings on vowel-initial words.
  - Omitting iota subscript.
  - Confusing eta with nu in handwriting.

CH 02 - BASIC SENTENCES
================================================================
Focus
  Present indicative active of regular omega-verbs; basic Greek
  sentence structure; subject implicit in the verb ending;
  negation with ou.

Key items
  - luw paradigm: luw, lueis, luei, luomen, luete, luousi(n).
  - Subject pronouns are usually carried by the ending; explicit
    nominative is for emphasis or contrast.
  - Word order is freer than English; verb-first is common.
  - Negation: ou before consonants, ouk before smooth-breathing
    vowels, ouch before rough-breathing vowels.
  - Movable nu on -ei / -si(n) endings before vowels or at
    sentence end.

Drill targets
  - Produce the full present indicative active of any -w verb
    given its lexical form.
  - Parse PAI forms: lexical form, T V M P N.
  - Translate short verb-only or verb + adverb sentences.

Common errors
  - Forgetting movable nu.
  - Confusing -ei (3sg) with -eis (2sg).
  - Using English subject pronouns when the ending already
    carries the subject.

CH 03 - CASES AND GENDER
================================================================
Focus
  The case system (nom, acc, gen, dat, voc); gender (m, f, n);
  number; the article; second declension. [verify whether 1st
  declension is in this chapter or split with ch 8]

Key items
  - Five cases and their core syntactic functions:
      Nom - subject; predicate nominative.
      Acc - direct object; extent of space / time; motion toward
            with prepositions.
      Gen - possession; source; separation; partitive; with
            certain prepositions and verbs.
      Dat - indirect object; location; instrument; with certain
            prepositions.
      Voc - direct address.
  - The article (ho, h, to) in all three genders, agreeing in
    case, number, and gender.
  - 2nd-declension masculines in -os (e.g. logos) and neuters
    in -on (e.g. ergon).
  - Neuter rule: a neuter plural subject typically takes a
    singular verb.

Drill targets
  - Full declension of 2nd-declension nouns and the article.
  - Identify case and function in short sentences.
  - "Why is this noun in this case?" prompts.

Common errors
  - Confusing the article (ho, h, hoi, hai) with the relative
    pronoun (hos, h, hoi, hai) - same shape, different
    accent / breathing.
  - Mixing genitive and dative endings -ou / -w.
  - Forgetting the neuter-plural / singular-verb agreement quirk.

CH 04 - PREPOSITIONS
================================================================
Focus
  Prepositions and the cases they govern; how case meaning
  interacts with preposition meaning.

Key items
  - One-case prepositions:
      en + dat (in, among).
      eis + acc (into, for).
      ek / ex + gen (out of).
      apo + gen (from).
      pro + gen (before).
      sun + dat (with).
  - Two-case prepositions (selected):
      dia: + gen (through), + acc (because of).
      meta: + gen (with), + acc (after).
      kata: + gen (down from, against), + acc (according to).
      huper: + gen (on behalf of), + acc (above, beyond).
  - Three-case prepositions: para, peri, epi, pros, hupo. [verify
    the exact set Duff introduces here vs. later chapters]
  - Elision before vowels: ap', di', met', etc.

Drill targets
  - Memorize preposition + case + meaning sets.
  - Translate phrases and short sentences featuring prepositional
    phrases.
  - Spot-check: given a preposition + noun, which case must the
    noun be in for this meaning?

Common errors
  - Choosing the wrong case for the intended meaning of a
    multi-case preposition.
  - Forgetting elision.
  - Treating en as if it took the accusative (English-driven).

CH 05 - ADJECTIVES
================================================================
Focus
  First- and second-declension adjectives; adjective-noun
  agreement; attributive, predicate, and substantival positions.

Key items
  - 2-1-2 adjectives: m and n on the 2nd declension, f on the 1st.
    Example: kalos, kalh, kalon.
  - Two-termination adjectives [verify whether introduced this
    chapter].
  - Agreement in case, number, and gender.
  - Attributive position: ho kalos logos / ho logos ho kalos
    ("the good word").
  - Predicate position: kalos ho logos / ho logos kalos
    ("the word is good"; supply esti).
  - Substantival adjective: ho kalos ("the good man"); to kalon
    ("the good thing"). Article + adjective, no noun.

Drill targets
  - Decline an adjective fully across all three genders.
  - Distinguish attributive from predicate position by article
    placement.
  - Translate sentences using each of the three positions.

Common errors
  - Mismatching gender between adjective and noun.
  - Translating predicate position as if attributive ("the good
    word" instead of "the word is good").
  - Forgetting that linking esti is often unwritten.

CH 06 - THE TENSES
================================================================
Focus
  Survey of the Greek tense system beyond the present:
  imperfect, future, aorist (1st and possibly 2nd) indicative
  active; explicit introduction of verbal aspect.

Key items
  - Imperfect active: augment + stem + secondary endings.
    eluon, elues, elue(n), eluomen, eluete, eluon.
  - Future active: stem + s + present-style endings.
    lusw, luseis, lusei, lusomen, lusete, lusousi(n).
  - First (sigmatic / weak) aorist active: augment + stem + sa +
    secondary endings. elusa, elusas, eluse(n), elusamen,
    elusate, elusan.
  - Second (strong) aorist active [verify whether in this
    chapter or in ch 11 / ch 19]: augment + altered stem +
    secondary endings. elabon, elabes, etc.
  - Aspect:
      Aorist = perfective (action viewed as a whole).
      Imperfect = imperfective in past (ongoing or repeated
        action).
      Present = imperfective in non-past.
      Future = aspectually neutral / underdetermined.
  - Augment moves to the front of the verb stem in compound
    verbs: apoluw -> apeluon.

Drill targets
  - Produce imperfect, future, and 1st aorist of a regular -w
    verb given its lexical form.
  - Parse forms with an explicit aspect statement, not just
    "past."
  - Translate sentences mixing tenses and have the student
    explain the aspect choice.

Common errors
  - Treating the aorist as simply "past" rather than as
    perfective aspect.
  - Forgetting the augment, especially in compound verbs.
  - Confusing 1st and 2nd aorist endings.

CH 08 - OTHER PATTERNS OF NOUNS AND VERBS
================================================================
Focus
  First-declension feminine and masculine nouns; contract verbs;
  eimi. [verify exact mix - some of these may sit in earlier
  chapters.]

Key items
  - First declension feminine: hmera (alpha-pure), doxa
    (alpha-impure), grafh (eta-stem).
  - First-declension masculine: profhths, neanias.
  - Contract verbs (-ew, -aw, -ow):
      -ew: e + e -> ei; e + o -> ou; e + w -> w.
      -aw: a + e -> a; a + o -> w.
      -ow: o + e -> ou; o + o -> ou.
  - eimi present indicative: eimi, ei, esti(n), esmen, este,
    eisi(n).
  - eimi imperfect: hmhn, hs, hn, hmen, hte, hsan.
  - eimi is enclitic in many forms; its imperfect is irregular
    (no augment in the standard sense).

Drill targets
  - Decline 1st-declension nouns in all three sub-types.
  - Conjugate poiew, agapaw, plhrow in present indicative active.
  - Recognize and produce eimi across persons, present and
    imperfect.

Common errors
  - Producing contract verbs in their uncontracted form.
  - Confusing eimi (be) with eimi/eisi forms in context.
  - Treating esti as the verb in a sentence where it must be
    supplied silently.

CH 09 - PRONOUNS AND CONJUNCTIONS
================================================================
Focus
  Personal, demonstrative, and relative pronouns; the high-
  frequency conjunctions and particles.

Key items
  - Personal pronouns: egw / hmeis; su / humeis; autos / auth /
    auto used as the 3rd-person pronoun in oblique cases.
  - autos has three syntactic uses:
      1) Oblique cases: 3rd-person pronoun ("him, her, it,
         them").
      2) Predicate position with article (ho autos logos):
         "the same word."
      3) Attributive / intensive: "the word itself" / "(he)
         himself."
  - Demonstratives: houtos / hauth / touto ("this");
    ekeinos / ekeinh / ekeino ("that").
  - Relative pronoun hos, h, ho. Distinguish from the article
    (ho, h, to) and from hoti.
  - Conjunctions and particles:
      kai (and).
      de (but / and; postpositive).
      gar (for; postpositive).
      oun (therefore; postpositive).
      alla (but).
      h (or).
      hoti (recitative; content "that"; causal "because").
      hina (in order that).
      ean (if, with subj).
      ei (if, with ind).
      hws (as, when, that).

Drill targets
  - Decline autos fully and use it in all three syntactic roles.
  - Distinguish article, relative pronoun, and hoti by accent /
    breathing / context.
  - Identify which conjunctions are postpositive and place them
    correctly in production.

Common errors
  - Treating autos in attributive position as if it were the
    oblique-case pronoun.
  - Confusing houtos with autos.
  - Forgetting that de, gar, oun cannot stand first in their
    clause.
  - Confusing relative hos with article ho.

CH 10 - COMPLEX SENTENCES
================================================================
Focus
  Subordinate clauses; conditional sentences (introduction);
  purpose with hina + subjunctive; indirect statement with
  hoti; result and reason. [verify exact mix]

Key items
  - Purpose: hina + subjunctive ("in order that ...").
  - Indirect statement: hoti + indicative ("[he said] that ...").
  - Conditional sentences (introduction):
      Real / present-real (1st class): ei + ind, ... ind.
      Future-more-vivid (3rd class): ean + subj, ... future ind.
      [Other types likely deferred to ch 20.]
  - Result clauses: hwste + indicative or infinitive [verify].
  - Causal: hoti / dioti / epei + indicative.

Drill targets
  - Identify a subordinate clause's type from its conjunction
    and verb mood.
  - Produce a Greek purpose clause from an English target.
  - Translate short conditional sentences and label the type.

Common errors
  - Putting an indicative in a hina purpose clause.
  - Confusing the three uses of hoti (recitative, content,
    causal).
  - Forgetting that hina clauses use the subjunctive.

CH 11 - SPECIAL VERBS
================================================================
Focus
  High-frequency irregular verbs and their principal parts.
  [verify whether mi-verbs are introduced here or in ch 19.]

Key items
  - The concept of principal parts (six forms generating every
    finite form a verb can take):
      1. Present (1sg act ind).
      2. Future (1sg act ind).
      3. Aorist active (1sg act ind).
      4. Perfect active (1sg act ind).
      5. Perfect middle / passive (1sg).
      6. Aorist passive (1sg).
  - Highly irregular high-frequency verbs:
      legw -> erw -> eipon -> eirhka -> eirhmai -> errethhn.
      erchomai -> eleusomai -> hlthon -> elhlutha -> -- -> --.
      horaw -> opsomai -> eidon -> hewraka -> hewramai ->
        wfthhn.
      ginwskw -> gnwsomai -> egnwn -> egnwka -> egnwsmai ->
        egnwsthhn.
      ferw -> oisw -> hnegka -> enhnocha -> enhnegmai ->
        hnechthhn.
      esthiw -> fagomai -> efagon -> -- -> -- -> --.
  - Suppletion: many of these "irregularities" are different
    roots fused into one paradigm.

Drill targets
  - Recite the six principal parts of each high-frequency
    irregular verb in scope.
  - Identify the principal part a given finite form derives
    from.
  - Translate sentences featuring these verbs in non-present
    tenses.

Common errors
  - Trying to derive an aorist by adding sa to an irregular
    root.
  - Missing that eipon is a 2nd aorist, not a present.
  - Treating hlthon as some kind of imperfect.

CH 12 - THIRD DECLENSION, PART 1
================================================================
Focus
  Third-declension nouns; consonant-stem patterns; case endings
  attaching to a stem ending in a stop.

Key items
  - Third-declension endings (canonical):
      Sg: -s / -- (nom), -a / -n (acc), -os (gen), -i (dat),
          (-) voc.
      Pl: -es (nom), -as (acc), -wn (gen), -si(n) (dat).
  - Consonant changes when stops meet -s or -si:
      k, g, ch + s -> x  (e.g. fulax / fulakos).
      p, b, f + s -> ps.
      t, d, th + s -> s  (the dental drops).
  - Common stop-stem nouns: ho fulax, fulakos; h elpis,
    elpidos; h nux, nuktos; to fws, fwtos.
  - The dative plural is the most diagnostic form for stop type.

Drill targets
  - Given a nom sg and gen sg, decline the noun fully.
  - Predict the dative plural from the stem ending.
  - Recognize a 3rd-declension form by its endings without
    knowing the stem.

Common errors
  - Reading 3rd-declension forms as if they were 2nd-decl
    masculines because of -os endings (which are gen sg here,
    not nom sg).
  - Failing to apply consonant changes in the dative plural.
  - Mistaking a 3rd-decl acc sg in -a for a feminine
    nominative.

CH 13 - THIRD DECLENSION, PART 2
================================================================
Focus
  Further 3rd-decl stem types: nasal stems, sigma stems, vowel
  stems; neuters in -ma(t-); irregular family-relation nouns.

Key items
  - Nasal stems: ho aiwn, aiwnos; ho poimhn, poimenos; ho
    hgemwn, hgemonos.
  - -nt stems: ho archwn, archontos (also relevant for the
    participle).
  - Sigma stems (neuters in -os): to genos, genous (genesos ->
    genous); to ethnos, ethnous.
  - Vowel stems: h polis, polews; ho basileus, basilews.
  - Neuters in -ma(t-): to onoma, onomatos; to pneuma,
    pneumatos; to thelhma, thelhmatos.
  - Irregulars: ho anhr, andros; h gunh, gunaikos; ho pathr,
    patros; h mhthr, mhtros; h thugathr, thugatros.

Drill targets
  - Decline at least one example of each stem type fully.
  - Recognize -ma as a strong cue for neuter 3rd-decl.
  - Drill the family-relation irregulars as a memorization set.

Common errors
  - Reading polis as if it followed elpis (different pattern).
  - Misaccenting pneumata.
  - Producing the gen sg of polis as polisos rather than polews.

CH 14 - PARTICIPLES
================================================================
Focus
  Full treatment of the participle: formation across tense and
  voice; declension; uses (attributive, substantival, adverbial,
  genitive absolute, periphrastic).

Key items
  - Formation:
      Present active: stem + wn / ousa / on (3rd-decl m & n,
        1st-decl f). luwn, luousa, luon.
      Present mid / pass: stem + omenos, h, on (2-1-2).
      1st aorist active: stem + sas, sasa, san.
      2nd aorist active: stem (altered) + wn, ousa, on.
      Aorist middle: samenos, h, on.
      Aorist passive: -theis, -theisa, -then.
      Perfect active: -ws, -uia, -os.
      Perfect mid / pass: -menos, h, on (with reduplication).
  - Aspectual force:
      Present participle - imperfective; contemporaneous with
        main verb.
      Aorist participle - perfective; often (not always) prior
        to main verb.
      Perfect participle - state resulting from a prior action.
  - Uses:
      Attributive (with article): "the man who is teaching" /
        "the teaching man."
      Substantival (with article, no noun): "the one teaching."
      Adverbial (no article): time, cause, manner, means,
        condition, concession.
      Genitive absolute: a participial clause with a noun /
        pronoun in the genitive that is not the subject of the
        main verb.
      Periphrastic: eimi + participle as an extended verbal
        form.

Drill targets
  - Decline a present active participle fully across all
    genders.
  - Identify the use of each participle in a given sentence
    (attributive / substantival / adverbial / gen abs).
  - Translate the same Greek sentence rendering an adverbial
    participle in two acceptable English ways.

Common errors
  - Reading every adverbial participle as "while X-ing"
    without considering cause, condition, etc.
  - Missing genitive absolutes because the genitive noun seems
    unrelated to the main verb.
  - Confusing aorist participles with finite aorists.
  - Translating present participles as past when the main verb
    is past.

CH 15 - THE PASSIVE AND VOICES
================================================================
Focus
  Voice in Greek: active, middle, passive. Formation of passive
  forms across tenses (esp. aorist passive in -th-). Deponents.

Key items
  - Active: subject acts.
  - Middle: subject acts on / for / with reference to itself.
  - Passive: subject is acted upon.
  - Forms:
      Present mid / pass: luomai, luh, luetai, luometha,
        luesthe, luontai. (Same form for middle and passive in
        present and imperfect.)
      Imperfect mid / pass: eluomhn, eluou, elueto, eluometha,
        eluesthe, eluonto.
      Future passive: -thsomai endings (luthhsomai, etc.).
      Aorist passive: -th- + secondary active endings (eluthhn,
        eluthhs, eluthh, eluthhmen, eluthhte, eluthhsan).
  - Agent in a passive construction:
      Personal: hupo + genitive ("by [a person]").
      Impersonal: dative of means ("by [a thing]").
  - Deponent verbs: middle / passive in form, active in meaning
    (e.g. erchomai, ginomai, dechomai, apokrinomai).

Drill targets
  - Conjugate luw fully in the passive across present,
    imperfect, aorist, future.
  - Recognize -th- as the diagnostic aorist-passive marker.
  - Translate passive sentences with both hupo + gen and
    dative of means.

Common errors
  - Reading aorist passive forms (eluthh) as if they were
    active because they take active-style endings.
  - Translating deponents as passives.
  - Missing the middle force in genuine middles (rare in NT but
    not nonexistent).

================================================================
CH 16 - THE PERFECT
================================================================
Focus
  Perfect and pluperfect indicative, active and middle / passive.
  Reduplication and its patterns.

Key items
  - Perfect active formation:
      Reduplication + stem + ka + primary endings.
      leluka, lelukas, leluke(n), lelukamen, lelukate,
        lelukasi(n).
  - Perfect mid / pass formation:
      Reduplication + stem (no -k-) + primary mid/pass endings.
      lelumai, lelusai, lelutai, lelumetha, lelusthe, leluntai.
  - Pluperfect active: augment + reduplication + stem + kei +
    secondary endings. (Less common in NT.)
  - Reduplication patterns:
      Consonant-initial stem: double the consonant + e
        (luw -> leluka).
      Vowel-initial stem: lengthen the vowel
        (akouw -> akhkoa - irregular).
      Aspirated-stop reduplication: deaspirated initial
        (filew -> pefilhka; thuw -> tethuka).
  - Aspect: the perfect denotes a state resulting from a
    completed action. Not simply a past tense.

Drill targets
  - Form the perfect of any regular -w verb given its stem.
  - Recognize reduplication as the diagnostic marker of perfect.
  - Translate perfects with English that captures the
    resultant-state nuance, not merely "have done."

Common errors
  - Mistaking reduplication for an augment.
  - Translating the perfect as a simple past.
  - Forgetting the absence of -k- in the perfect mid / pass.

================================================================
CH 17 - THE SUBJUNCTIVE
================================================================
Focus
  Full treatment of the subjunctive mood: formation and uses.

Key items
  - Formation: long thematic vowel (h / w) replaces the short
    one of the indicative; primary endings remain.
      Present active subj: luw, luhs, luh, luwmen, luhte,
        luwsi(n).
      Aorist active subj: lusw, lushs, lush, luswmen, lushte,
        luswsi(n). (No augment in subjunctive.)
      Aorist passive subj: luthw, luthhs, luthh, luthwmen,
        luthhte, luthwsi(n).
  - Aspect, not time: present subj = imperfective; aorist subj
    = perfective. Both are non-past in subjunctive.
  - Uses:
      Hortatory ("let us X"): present or aorist subj, 1pl.
      Deliberative question ("am I to X?"): subj, 1sg or 1pl.
      Prohibition: mh + aorist subj (2nd person), or mh + present
        imperative.
      Purpose: hina / hopws + subj.
      Indefinite / general clauses: hotan, hews an, hostis an,
        etc., + subj.
      Future-more-vivid conditional: ean + subj, ... future ind.
      Strong denial: ou mh + aorist subj.

Drill targets
  - Conjugate the present and aorist subjunctive of a regular
    verb.
  - Identify the use of a subjunctive in a given sentence by
    the subordinator.
  - Translate hina and hotan clauses with full attention to the
    subjunctive's modal force.

Common errors
  - Forgetting that the subjunctive has no augment.
  - Confusing 2sg pres ind active (lueis) with 2sg pres subj
    active (luhs) - different vowel and accent.
  - Translating hina + subj as English purpose without
    preserving modal nuance.

CH 18 - USING VERBS
================================================================
Focus
  Synthesis of the verbal system; the infinitive; the
  imperative; further work on aspect; complex verbal
  constructions. [verify exact mix]

Key items
  - Infinitive forms:
      Present active: -ein (luein).
      Aorist active: -sai (lusai).
      Aorist passive: -thnai (luthhnai).
      Perfect active: -kenai (lelukenai).
      Mid / pass: -esthai, -sasthai, -sthai.
  - Uses of the infinitive:
      Complementary (with a finite verb): thelw luein.
      Articular (preceded by tou, en tw, eis to, etc.) for
        purpose, time, etc.
      Indirect statement (after verbs of saying / believing /
        commanding): subject of the inf in the accusative.
  - Imperative:
      Present active 2sg / 2pl: lue, luete.
      Aorist active 2sg / 2pl: luson, lusate.
      3rd-person imperative endings: -tw, -twsan / -ntwn.
      Negative imperative (prohibition):
        mh + present imperative ("stop doing X" / "don't be
          doing X").
        mh + aorist subjunctive ("don't start doing X").
  - Aspect with non-finite forms: present = imperfective,
    aorist = perfective; aspect dominates time outside the
    indicative.

Drill targets
  - Form all four infinitive types of a regular verb.
  - Distinguish complementary vs. articular vs. indirect-
    statement uses.
  - Form imperatives across both aspects and recognize the
    semantic difference.

Common errors
  - Translating an aorist infinitive as past.
  - Treating an articular infinitive as if it were a noun-only
    construction without noting verbal force.
  - Forgetting that the subject of an indirect-statement
    infinitive is accusative.

CH 19 - EXTRA VERBS
================================================================
Focus
  mi-verbs and other paradigmatically "extra" verbs (liquid
  futures, athematic forms, irregular aorists). [verify scope]

Key items
  - The four most common mi-verbs:
      didwmi ("I give") - didwmi, didws, didwsi(n), didomen,
        didote, didoasi(n). Aorist: edwka.
      tithhmi ("I place / put") - tithhmi, tithhs, tithhsi(n),
        tithemen, tithete, titheasi(n). Aorist: ethhka.
      histhmi ("I make stand / [intransitive] stand") -
        histhmi, hisths, histhsi(n), ... Aorist (transitive)
        esthsa; (intransitive) esthn. Note the dual aorist
        system.
      afihmi ("I let go / forgive") - compound of hihmi.
  - Athematic endings: mi-verbs add endings directly to a stem
    that ends in a vowel (no thematic o / e).
  - Liquid futures: stems ending in l, m, n, r form the future
    with -ew contraction rather than -sw. menw -> menw (with
    circumflex on the final syllable, distinct from present).
  - Liquid aorists: stems in l, m, n, r form the aorist with
    lengthening of the stem vowel rather than sa. apostellw ->
    apesteila.

Drill targets
  - Recite the present indicative active of the four common
    mi-verbs.
  - Recite the principal parts of didwmi and tithhmi.
  - Recognize and parse mi-verb forms in NT-style sentences.
  - Distinguish transitive esthsa from intransitive esthn.

Common errors
  - Trying to read mi-verb forms with thematic-vowel
    expectations.
  - Reading 3pl in -asi(n) (didoasin) as a 3pl -w form.
  - Confusing the two aorists of histhmi.
  - Producing liquid futures sigmatically (*menso instead of
    menw).

CH 20 - FINAL PIECES
================================================================
Focus
  Wrap-up topics: numerals, more pronouns, the full conditional
  system, indirect speech, idioms, reading skills. [verify
  exact contents.]

Key items (likely)
  - Numerals: cardinal one through twelve; the irregular
    declension of heis, mia, hen and of treis / tessares;
    ordinals prwtos, deuteros, etc.
  - Indefinite vs. interrogative: tis / ti (indef, enclitic) vs.
    tis / ti (interrog, accented).
  - Reflexive pronouns: emautou, seautou, heautou.
  - Conditional sentences - full system:
      1st class (real / present-real): ei + ind, ind.
      2nd class (contrary-to-fact): augmented forms in both
        clauses, an in apodosis. "If X were the case (it isn't),
        Y would be."
      3rd class (future-more-vivid): ean + subj, future ind.
      4th class (future-less-vivid; rare in NT): ei + opt,
        opt + an.
  - Indirect statement, indirect command, indirect question -
    synthesis.
  - Reading idioms and common particles in narrative.

Drill targets
  - Decline heis / treis / tessares fully.
  - Distinguish all four classes of conditional in NT context.
  - Translate continuous narrative passages of NT-style Greek.

Common errors
  - Confusing tis (indef) with tis (interrog) on the page.
  - Mislabeling 3rd-class conditionals as 1st-class because of
    surface similarity.
  - Translating contrary-to-fact conditionals as future hopes.
